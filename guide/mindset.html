Mindset

February 2026 · Part of the LLM Guide for the Humanities & Social Sciences

Last updated: February 2026

This page draws on advice from the AI Daily Brief podcast episode on 'How to Learn AI with AI', adapted here for a humanities audience.

Why mindset matters

Most technology works the same way every time. A spreadsheet formula either calculates correctly or it does not. A library database returns the same results for the same query. You learn the interface, follow the steps, and get predictable outcomes.

Large language models are different. They are generative, non-deterministic, and responsive to how you interact with them. The same question asked in two different ways can produce substantially different answers. This means that the habits you have developed for using other software — following procedures, minimising input, expecting consistency — will actively hold you back. Getting good results from an LLM requires a different kind of engagement: more conversational, more iterative, and more willing to experiment.

The shifts described below are not technical skills. They are habits of interaction — and for humanities scholars, many of them will feel surprisingly familiar. Treating AI as a seminar partner rather than a search engine turns out to be closer to the truth of how these systems work.

Foundational Mindset Shifts

1. Begin with your scholarly question, not the tool

The most common mistake is opening an LLM and wondering what to do with it. Instead, start from a problem you already have — a translation you are wrestling with, a lecture that needs restructuring, a grant application that needs sharpening — and then ask whether the tool can help with some part of it. Think about what you would ask a capable research assistant or a colleague in a neighbouring discipline. If you can articulate that, you already have a good prompt.

The people who get the most from these tools are not the ones who learn elaborate prompting techniques. They are the ones who bring clear questions from their own work.

2. Embrace messy, exploratory thinking

Academic training rewards polished output. We hesitate to commit half-formed ideas to writing, and we instinctively tidy up our thinking before presenting it. With an LLM, this instinct works against you.

These systems respond well to rough, exploratory input. You can think out loud, share provisional ideas, dump unstructured notes, and ask the system to help you find the shape of an argument. You do not need to know exactly what you want before you start. In fact, some of the most productive uses of these tools come from what software developers call rubber-duck debugging — a practice where simply explaining a problem out loud (originally to a rubber duck on your desk) helps you see it more clearly. The LLM serves this function, with the added benefit that it can respond, ask clarifying questions, and suggest angles you had not considered.

This is closer to the generative phase of seminar discussion than to the polished phase of a published article. Treat early interactions as a thinking space, not a production line.

3. Push back rigorously

LLMs are cooperative by default. They tend to agree with you, elaborate on your premises, and produce confident-sounding responses even when they are uncertain. This is useful when you need fluent prose; it is dangerous when you need critical scrutiny.

The solution is to push back explicitly. Tell the system: “Challenge this argument.” “What are the strongest objections?” “Where is this reasoning weakest?” “You are being too agreeable — I need genuine critique.” You can also assign it a specific critical perspective: “Respond as a sceptical reviewer for [journal].” “Argue the opposite position.”

This is not a flaw to work around — it is a feature to use deliberately. The system can adopt critical stances that are genuinely useful for stress-testing your work, but only if you ask it to. Left to its defaults, it will tell you your draft is excellent.

4. Let the AI draft widely, then curate

A common frustration is that the first response is not quite right. This leads people to conclude the tool is not useful for their purposes. But the first response is rarely the point.

Ask for multiple options: “Give me five different ways to frame this argument.” “Suggest three possible structures for this chapter.” “Draft four opening paragraphs in different registers.” Then select the elements that work and refine from there. This is closer to how you might use a brainstorming session — generating material broadly before narrowing down — than to how you use a reference tool.

Your scholarly judgement is what makes the output valuable. The system generates; you evaluate, select, and reshape.

5. Use AI as an intellectual mirror

One of the less obvious uses is turning the system into a sounding board for your own thinking. Ask it to summarise what you have said, to identify the implicit assumptions in your argument, or to explain your position back to you as though for a different audience.

This is particularly useful when you are too close to your material — when you have been working on a problem for so long that you can no longer see where your reasoning depends on unstated premises. The system’s summary of your argument is often revealing precisely because it reflects what you actually said rather than what you meant.

6. Zoom out regularly

Long conversations with an LLM tend to narrow. The system follows your lead, and as you drill into details, the broader context can get lost. Periodically ask the system to step back: “Summarise where we have got to.” “What is the main argument we have been building?” “Are we still addressing the original question?”

This is especially important in extended working sessions. It is easy to spend an hour refining a paragraph that no longer serves the argument, or to follow an interesting tangent that has drifted away from what you actually need. The system will not flag this on its own — it follows your direction — so the responsibility for maintaining perspective rests with you.

7. Know when to stop a thread and start fresh

Sometimes a conversation goes in the wrong direction and corrections are not fixing it. The system may have latched onto a misunderstanding early on and is now building on flawed premises, or the conversation has accumulated so many qualifications and redirections that the context is muddled.

When this happens, it is often more productive to start a new conversation with a clearer prompt than to keep correcting course. This is not a failure — it is efficient use of the tool. The work you did in the first conversation helped you clarify what you actually want; the second conversation benefits from that clarity.

Practical Techniques

1. Maintain handoff documents

LLMs do not remember previous conversations (unless memory features are enabled — see Setting Up). If you are working on an ongoing project across multiple sessions, you will lose context each time you start a new conversation.

The solution is to keep a running document — a handoff note — that summarises where you are: what has been decided, what is still open, what the current state of the work is. Paste this at the start of each new session. This takes a few minutes but saves considerable time in re-establishing context. Think of it as the minutes of a meeting: a record that lets everyone (including the AI) pick up where they left off.

2. Use project workspaces where available

Most LLM platforms now offer some form of persistent project or workspace feature (Claude calls them “Projects”; ChatGPT uses “Custom GPTs” or similar). These allow you to upload reference documents, set standing instructions, and maintain context across sessions without re-pasting everything each time.

If you are working on a book chapter, a module redesign, or a grant application over several weeks, setting up a dedicated workspace is worth the five minutes it takes. Upload your key documents, set instructions about your preferred style and approach, and let the system carry that context forward.

3. Share exact content, not descriptions of content

If you want feedback on a draft, paste the draft. If you want help with a translation, paste the passage. If you want the system to understand your institution’s assessment policy, paste the policy. Do not describe what it says — give the system the actual text.

LLMs work much better with concrete material than with second-hand summaries. “Here is my draft introduction — how could the argument be tightened?” will produce far more useful feedback than “I have written an introduction about X — any advice?”

4. Use AI to write prompts for other tools

This is a surprisingly practical technique. If you need to use a tool you are unfamiliar with — a database query language, a citation manager’s advanced search syntax, a spreadsheet formula — ask the LLM to write the query or formula for you. You can describe what you want in plain language and get back the technical syntax.

This also works for writing prompts for other AI tools. If you are trying to get a specific result from an image generator, a transcription tool, or a different LLM, you can ask your current system to help you craft an effective prompt. It is tools all the way down, and there is no reason not to use one tool to get more out of another.

5. Do not start over impulsively

When a response is not what you wanted, the instinct is to start a new conversation. Resist this. Specific feedback within the current conversation is almost always more effective: “The second paragraph assumes X, but actually Y.” “This is too general — focus on the specific case of Z.” “Good structure, wrong tone — this should be more formal.”

The system builds on the conversation history. Correcting course preserves the useful context you have already established. Starting fresh throws that away. (This is not a contradiction with point 7 above — there are times when starting fresh is the right call, but it should be a deliberate decision, not a reflex.)

6. Consider voice input

Most LLM platforms support voice input, and for many people it changes the dynamic of interaction significantly. Speaking a rough idea is faster and more natural than typing it, and it encourages the kind of exploratory, unpolished input that these systems work best with.

This is particularly useful for the “thinking out loud” mode described above. If you find yourself staring at a blank prompt, try saying what you are thinking rather than typing it. The result may be less tidy, but that is often an advantage — you are giving the system more to work with. The Fundamental Shift

The traditional model for learning software is: read the manual, learn the features, follow the steps, get predictable results. Expertise means knowing the right procedure.

Working with LLMs is different. Expertise means knowing how to communicate what you need, how to evaluate what you get back, and how to iterate toward something useful. It is less like learning to use a tool and more like learning to work with a new colleague — one who is knowledgeable but sometimes unreliable, always willing but never independently critical, and capable of excellent work when well directed.

For humanities scholars, this should be encouraging. The skills that matter most — clear articulation of complex questions, critical evaluation of arguments, sensitivity to nuance and register, willingness to revise — are skills you already have. The challenge is not learning a new technical vocabulary. It is recognising that the skills you use in seminars, supervision, and peer review are precisely the skills that make these tools useful.
