Getting Started

February 2026 · Part of the LLM Guide for Humanities Scholars

Last updated: February 2026

Try It Yourself

This exercise takes about fifteen minutes. Its purpose is not to produce something useful but to calibrate your own sense of what these tools do well and where they fall short. You are the expert here. The point is to test the system against material you know, so you can form a grounded judgement rather than relying on other people’s impressions.

Primary exercise: translation

Choose a short passage (3–5 sentences) in a language you read well — something syntactically demanding, where interpretive choices matter. The more you know the passage, the better: the point is to test the tool against material where you can judge quality.

  1. Open an LLM. Go to claude.ai (or chatgpt.com if you prefer). A free account is fine for this exercise.
  2. Paste the passage and ask: “Please translate this into English.”
  3. Read the translation carefully. Note what works and what doesn’t. Does it capture the style and register of the original, or does it smooth everything into generic fluent prose? Where has it made interpretive choices, and are they the ones you would make?
  4. Now try: “What alternative renderings are possible for [a specific phrase]?” Choose a phrase where the translation involves genuine interpretive stakes. See whether the system can articulate what those stakes are, or whether it simply offers synonyms.
  5. Ask: “What is the rhetorical or literary function of this passage in the wider work?”
  6. Assess the response against what you know. Where does its analysis feel generic? Where does it show genuine purchase on the material? What would a student miss that you can see?

What you’ll produce: A calibrated judgement of how the tool handles material in your domain — where it performs well, where it falls short, and what the failure modes look like.

How to check it: Compare the translation against a published rendering or your own. Identify at least one place where the LLM missed nuance that matters.

Common pitfalls: Accepting a fluent translation as adequate without checking the interpretive choices. Concluding the tool is “good enough” based on a passage you know well — the real test is material at the edge of your expertise, where errors are harder to spot.

The value of this exercise lies in the assessment, not the output. You already know this text, or texts like it. What you are discovering is how the tool handles material where you can judge quality—which is the only reliable way to estimate how it will perform on material where you cannot.

Alternative exercise: discussion questions

If you do not work with ancient languages, try this instead. Pick a topic from your current teaching—a seminar theme, a set text, a problem you regularly ask students to grapple with. Ask the system: “Generate six discussion questions for a postgraduate seminar on [your topic].” Then evaluate the results. Which questions would actually provoke useful discussion with your students? Which ones miss the point of why the topic matters in your discipline? What do the gaps tell you about how the system understands—or fails to understand—your field?

Bring your own material: For either exercise, you can substitute material from your own work — a tricky passage from a primary source, a dense paragraph from a secondary text, or a section of your own writing that needs restructuring. The same principle applies: test the tool against material where you can judge quality.

Setting Up

Choosing a platform

The main consumer platforms are Claude (Anthropic), ChatGPT (OpenAI), and Gemini (Google). All offer free tiers with older models and usage limits; paid subscriptions (£16–25/month) provide access to more capable models and higher limits.

This guide defaults to Claude, partly because it is what we know best and partly because Anthropic’s approach to safety and transparency aligns reasonably with academic values—but that is a judgement call, not an endorsement. If you are going to experiment seriously, a paid tier is worth it: the difference between free and frontier models is substantial, and struggling with an underpowered model produces misleading impressions of what the technology can and cannot do.

Current frontier models shift regularly. What matters is accessing whatever your chosen platform considers its most capable offering. Check the platform’s model selector or documentation for the latest options. Using an older or free-tier model will give you a misleading impression of what the technology can do.

Privacy and training settings

By default, many platforms use your conversations to train future models. Most offer settings to opt out. In Claude: Settings → Privacy → disable the use of your conversations for training. This prevents your inputs being incorporated into training data, though they still pass through commercial servers.

The key principle: do not input anything you would be uncomfortable with a company storing, even temporarily. If your university provides institutional access (through Microsoft Copilot or a managed LLM account), the data protection provisions are typically stronger than consumer accounts. Check with your IT services.

For the full picture on data boundaries — what to avoid inputting, institutional vs consumer accounts, and the practical test to apply — see Rules, Risks & Verification.

Memory settings

Current systems can maintain memory across conversations—tracking your preferences, ongoing projects, and context over time. You can view, edit, and delete what the system remembers.

Whether to enable this depends on your use patterns. If you are using the tool for ongoing projects where context continuity helps, memory reduces repetitive re-prompting. If you prefer each conversation to start fresh, or if you are uncomfortable with persistent profiles being built, disable it.

Review memory contents periodically if enabled. Systems sometimes remember things inaccurately or retain information you would prefer they did not.

Prompting Principles

How you phrase a request shapes what you get back. This is not about memorising formulae or mastering a technical skill—it is about communicating clearly with a system that responds to specificity, context, and structure. The following principles will improve your results immediately.

Specificity beats generality. Not “tell me about Virgil” but “identify three passages in Aeneid 6 where Virgil engages with Lucretian material, and explain what is at stake in each engagement.” The more precisely you define what you want, the less work you do filtering generic output. Vague prompts produce vague responses; pointed prompts produce responses you can actually evaluate.

Context constraints reshape outputs. Tell the system what to exclude or what lens to adopt: “Without using psychoanalytic frameworks.” “Focusing on material evidence rather than literary sources.” “Assuming a reader sceptical of computational methods.” These constraints do not guarantee the system will honour them perfectly, but they substantially narrow the output toward what you need.

Examples calibrate expectations. If you want a particular structure, tone, or approach, show the system one concrete instance of what you mean. A single example does more to shape output than several paragraphs of abstract instruction. This is especially useful for formatting tasks, stylistic preferences, and disciplinary conventions the system may not infer from description alone.

Ask for alternatives. “Give me three different approaches” produces more useful material than asking for “the” answer. Multiple options let you compare, combine, and select—which is a better use of both the system’s generative capacity and your own judgement. A single response invites acceptance or rejection; several responses invite critical evaluation.

Iterate with specific feedback. “This section works because X—revise the rest to match.” “You are treating the corpus as uniform, but periodisation matters.” Do not start a new conversation when the current one is going in the wrong direction—correct course. The system responds well to specific critique. Tell it what went wrong and why, and the next attempt will usually be closer to what you need.

None of this requires technical expertise. It requires the same skills you already use when briefing a research assistant, writing examination questions, or structuring a seminar: clarity about what you want, awareness of what you are likely to get, and willingness to push back when the first attempt falls short.
