Rules, Risks & Verification

February 2026 · Part of the LLM Guide for Humanities Scholars

1. Data boundaries

Every interaction with a commercial LLM sends your input to external servers. Even when you opt out of training, your data is processed, temporarily stored, and potentially accessible to the provider for safety monitoring. The practical question is: what level of exposure is acceptable for this material?

Never input:

  • Personal data about identifiable individuals (student records, personnel matters, patient data)
  • Embargoed or restricted research data
  • Material subject to ethics approval or access restrictions
  • Credentials, passwords, or access tokens
  • Confidential committee or governance documents

These should not go into any commercial LLM regardless of privacy settings or institutional agreements.

Institutional vs consumer accounts

If your university provides access through a procurement agreement (increasingly common in UK HE), the privacy terms differ significantly from consumer accounts. Institutional agreements typically include stronger data protection provisions, GDPR compliance commitments, and guarantees that data will not be used for training. Check with your IT services or data protection officer.

Microsoft 365 Copilot operates within your institution’s existing data tenancy and inherits its security policies. For work that must stay within institutional boundaries, this is usually the appropriate tool. See the Copilot page for details.

Consumer accounts with training disabled are a reasonable interim for non-sensitive work. Disable training use in your platform settings (in Claude: Settings → Privacy). This prevents your conversations being used to train future models but does not make them private in an absolute sense.

The practical test

Before pasting anything into an LLM, ask: would I be comfortable with a commercial company storing this text, even temporarily? If the answer gives you pause, do not input it — or work with it in an institutional tool where the data governance is established.

2. The verification ladder

All LLM output requires verification. The question is how much. These three tiers match verification effort to stakes.

Tier 1: Quick check (routine work, internal use)
Ask the model to flag its own uncertainty and provide alternatives. Scan the output for obvious errors. Suitable for: drafting emails, generating discussion questions, reformatting data, internal notes.

Tier 2: Moderate verification (shared or semi-public work)
Require structured outputs — claim, evidence, source, confidence level. Spot-check sources against primary materials. Cross-check key claims with a second model or authoritative reference. Suitable for: teaching materials, grant drafts, committee papers, preliminary analysis.

Tier 3: Publication-grade verification (anything you will publish, submit, or defend)
Verify all citations exist and say what is claimed. Cross-check every factual claim against authoritative sources. For translations, compare against published scholarship and your own reading. Treat AI output as a hypothesis to test, not a draft to polish. Suitable for: journal articles, book chapters, grant submissions, formal reports.

Citation verification

Assume all citations are potentially hallucinated until verified. Check that:

  • The source exists
  • The author and title match
  • The page numbers contain what is claimed
  • The source actually supports the point being made

This is non-negotiable for anything you will publish or submit.

Cross-model checking

Querying multiple models (Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini) can surface errors. But cross-model agreement is not validation — it is correlated plausibility. Models trained on overlapping data make overlapping errors. Agreement on a factual claim means it is well-represented in training data, not that it is true. Cross-model review is most valuable when models disagree: that tells you where to focus your expert attention. See the cross-checking section on the Agentic AI page for a fuller discussion.

Translation checking

For ancient languages, compare outputs across models and against published translations. Divergences flag passages requiring expert attention. Never treat any model’s translation as authoritative. See Ancient Languages for worked examples.

The verification paradox

If you can verify what an LLM provides, you might not have needed it for that task. The tool’s value lies where checking is faster than creating from scratch, or where outputs prompt your own thinking rather than substituting for it. If verification burden exceeds generation benefit, use different methods.

3. Provenance and disclosure

When to disclose

Major publishers and funding bodies increasingly require disclosure of generative AI use. The Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE) is explicit that AI tools cannot be listed as authors, since authorship requires accountability. A reasonable default:

  • Disclose when AI shaped the substance of the work: research design, analysis, drafting, translation
  • Disclosure is unnecessary for trivial uses: spell-checking, formatting, grammar correction
  • When in doubt, disclose — in methods or acknowledgements

Check the specific policies of your target venue. Requirements vary and are evolving.

Recording your process

For research you intend to publish or defend, keep a record of:

  • Which model and version you used
  • The key prompts you gave
  • What the output was and how you modified it
  • What verification you performed

For agentic work (where the AI modified files, ran code, or transformed data), also save the agent’s log of actions and the state of your files before and after. Consider depositing these records in a data repository such as Zenodo as part of a technical appendix.

Responsibility

AI involvement does not diminish intellectual accountability. You are responsible for everything you publish or submit, regardless of how it was produced. If an AI-generated citation turns out to be fabricated, the error is yours. If an AI-assisted translation contains a significant misreading, the responsibility is yours. This is not a change from existing norms — it is the same standard applied to a new tool.

4. When not to use these tools

Not every task benefits from AI assistance. Prefer manual work or other methods when:

  • Verification would take longer than doing the task yourself. If checking 50 agent-modified files takes longer than making the changes by hand, the tool is not saving you anything.
  • You cannot verify the output’s accuracy. If you lack the expertise to check whether the result is correct, the system’s confident output may mislead you.
  • The material is confidential. Sensitive data should not pass through commercial servers.
  • Slow engagement is the point. Close reading, interpretive judgement, and the friction of drafting from scratch are themselves scholarly practices, not overhead to be optimised away.
  • The task requires judgement at every step. If the task is the judgement — editorial decisions, source evaluation, interpretive argument — delegation does not work.
  • You do not have time to review the output. An unchecked AI output is worse than none, because it creates the appearance of completed work.

The question is not whether these tools can produce output for a given task, but whether using them actually improves your work. Often the answer is no, and that is fine.

5. Templates and checklists

These are designed to be copied and adapted for your own use. Select the text in any box below and paste it into your own documents.

System prompt templates

Set these as persistent instructions in your LLM platform (in Claude: Settings → Profile) to shape all conversations.

For teaching:

I am a humanities academic preparing teaching materials for university students. When I ask about primary sources, cite specific passages. Flag uncertainty rather than guessing. Suggest where students might struggle with the material. Avoid theoretical frameworks I have not specifically requested. Use British English. When generating discussion questions, range from comprehension to interpretation and include at least one question that challenges common assumptions.

For research:

I am a researcher in [your field]. When I ask about sources, cite specific passages and flag when you are uncertain. Challenge my interpretations rather than agreeing reflexively. When I ask for counterarguments, give me the strongest versions, not strawmen. Distinguish clearly between what a source says and your inference from it. If I ask you to translate, flag ambiguous or contested readings rather than smoothing them over. Use British English.

For administration:

I am a university academic. Help me with administrative tasks: drafting emails, committee papers, reports, and policy documents. Use a professional but not corporate tone. Be concise. When I share a draft, suggest structural improvements and flag anything unclear or potentially contentious. Do not add unnecessary qualifications or caveats. Use British English.

Citation verification checklist

For each AI-generated citation, check:

  1. Does this publication exist? (Search library catalogue, Google Scholar, or publisher website)
  2. Are the author name, title, and date correct?
  3. Do the page numbers exist and contain what is claimed?
  4. Does the source actually support the point being made?
  5. Is there a more authoritative or recent source for this claim?

AI use disclosure statement

For your own publications:

[Model name and version] was used to assist with [drafting / translation / data processing / literature review scaffolding]. All substantive claims, interpretations, and conclusions are the author’s own. Citations were independently verified against primary sources. The author takes full responsibility for the content.

For student assignments (suggested policy language):

If you use generative AI tools in preparing this assignment, you must: (1) declare which tools you used and for what purpose; (2) include the key prompts you gave; (3) explain how you verified the output; (4) demonstrate your own critical engagement with the material. AI-generated text submitted without declaration constitutes an academic integrity violation. Note: you remain responsible for the accuracy and quality of everything you submit.

Agent audit record

Complete for each significant agentic task:

  • Task description: [What you asked the agent to do]
  • Model and tool: [e.g. Claude Opus 4.6 via Claude Code]
  • Scope constraints: [Which files/folders; read-only vs read-write; internet access]
  • Pre-task snapshot: [Git commit hash, or “folder copy saved to [location]”]
  • Sampling plan: [How many items checked by hand; which ones; what you looked for]
  • Results: [What the agent produced; errors found; corrections made]
  • Post-task state: [Git commit hash, or description of final state]
