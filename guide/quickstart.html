What are these tools?

Large Language Models (LLMs) generate text by predicting statistically probable sequences of words based on patterns in their training data. They are not knowledge bases, not reasoning engines, and do not understand what they produce — but they can generate text that is remarkably useful for some tasks and remarkably misleading for others. The only way to discover where those boundaries lie for your work is to try.

15-Minute Exercise

You are the expert here. The point is not whether the tool gives a perfect answer, but what you discover about its capabilities and limitations when applied to material you know well.

Option A: Translation (if you work with texts in other languages)

  1. Go to claude.ai (or chatgpt.com). A free account is fine.
  2. Paste a short passage (3–5 sentences) in a language you read well — something syntactically demanding, where interpretive choices matter.
  3. Ask: “Please translate this into English.”
  4. Assess the translation. Does it capture the style and register? Where has it made interpretive choices? What is lost?
  5. Now try: “What alternative renderings are possible for [a specific phrase]?”
  6. Ask: “What is the rhetorical or literary function of this passage in the wider work?”
  7. Judge the response against what you know. Where does it show genuine purchase? Where is it generic?

Option B: Discussion questions (if you don’t work with ancient languages)

Pick a topic from your current teaching. Ask: “Generate six discussion questions for a postgraduate seminar on [your topic].” Which questions would actually work? Which miss the point? What do the gaps tell you?

The goal is not to produce something useful — it is to calibrate your own sense of what these tools do well and where they fall short.

Key Terms

LLM (Large Language Model)
AI systems trained on vast text to predict and generate language. Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini are LLMs.
Hallucination
When a model generates false information presented as fact — invented citations, wrong dates, fictional events described confidently.
Prompt
The input you give to a model. How you phrase it shapes what you get back.
Context window
The amount of text a model can process at once. Current models handle roughly 75,000–150,000 words.
Token
The unit models use to process text — roughly one word. Relevant to usage limits and pricing.
Frontier model
The most capable currently available models. Changes as new models release.
RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)
A technique that grounds model responses in your own documents rather than general training data.
Fine-tuning
Additional training on specific data to specialise a model for particular tasks.
Guardrails
Restrictions built into AI systems to prevent harmful outputs.
Multimodal
Models that process text, images, audio, and documents — not just text.
