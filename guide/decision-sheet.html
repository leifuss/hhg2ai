AI Decision Sheet

February 2026 · Part of the LLM Guide for Humanities Scholars

Last updated: February 2026

A one-page reference for deciding whether and how to use AI for a specific task. Print this or keep it open while working. For the full guidance behind each step, see Rules, Risks & Verification.

This page is designed to print cleanly on a single sheet. Use your browser’s print function (Ctrl+P / Cmd+P) to save or print it.

1. What am I trying to do?

Name the specific task. Be precise — “draft six discussion questions for my Week 4 seminar” is better than “help with teaching.”

Ask: Would verification take longer than doing this myself? If yes, do it yourself. If no, proceed.

2. What data is involved?

Never input:
  • Personal data about identifiable people
  • Embargoed or restricted research data
  • Material under ethics approval
  • Credentials or access tokens
  • Confidential governance documents
Choose your tool:
  • Sensitive institutional data → Institutional tool only (e.g. Copilot within your tenancy)
  • Non-sensitive academic work → Any tool, training disabled
  • Published / public material → Any tool

Test: Would I be comfortable with a commercial company storing this text, even temporarily?

3. What are the stakes?

Tier 1 — Quick check
Internal, routine use
Scan for obvious errors. Ask the model to flag uncertainty.
Emails, discussion questions, reformatting, notes.
Tier 2 — Moderate verification
Shared or semi-public
Spot-check sources. Cross-check key claims. Require structured outputs.
Teaching materials, grant drafts, committee papers.
Tier 3 — Publication-grade
Anything you will publish, submit, or defend
Verify all citations exist and say what is claimed. Cross-check every factual claim. Treat output as a hypothesis to test.
Journal articles, book chapters, grant submissions, formal reports.

4. What must I log?

For anything you will publish or submit, record:

  • Which model and version
  • Key prompts given
  • What the output was and how you modified it
  • What verification you performed

Disclose when AI shaped the substance. When in doubt, disclose — in methods or acknowledgements. Check your publisher’s and institution’s specific policies.

5. Before I use the output…

  • Have I applied the appropriate verification tier?
  • Have I checked all citations against primary sources?
  • Am I treating this as a hypothesis I tested, not a draft I polished?
  • Could I defend every claim if challenged?
  • Have I recorded the process for disclosure?
