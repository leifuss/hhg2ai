Frequently Asked Questions

February 2026 · Part of the LLM Guide for Humanities Scholars

Last updated: February 2026

These are questions colleagues commonly raise. Each answer is deliberately brief — the guide's other pages go deeper where relevant.

"Will this replace us?"

No. These systems generate text, not scholarship. They cannot formulate research questions, evaluate evidence within disciplinary traditions, make interpretive judgements, or do the slow, uncertain work of original thinking. What may change is which tasks are done how — some routine work may become faster, and some skills currently valued may shift in importance. But the core of what humanities scholars do — critical interpretation of human culture — requires exactly the kind of judgement these systems lack. The more realistic concern is not replacement but institutional pressure to adopt these tools in ways that degrade the quality of teaching and research. That's worth resisting, but it's a policy question, not a technology inevitability.

"Can students use this to cheat?"

Yes, and some will. This is a genuine problem without easy solutions. Detection tools are unreliable and produce false positives that harm honest students. The more productive responses involve assessment design — oral examinations, process-based assessment, in-class writing, asking students to engage with specific sources in ways that require actual reading. But this is not a new problem in kind, only in degree. Students have always been able to outsource work; these tools lower the barrier. The conversation worth having is about what our assessments actually test and whether they still test it effectively. The briefing discusses this further.

"Is it ethical to use this?"

That depends on what you mean and what you value. There are genuine ethical concerns — environmental costs, exploitative labour practices, training data consent, concentration of corporate power. These are laid out in the briefing's ethics section. None of them have clean answers. Choosing not to use these tools for ethical reasons is entirely defensible. So is choosing measured engagement while remaining aware of the trade-offs. What's less defensible is either uncritical adoption or dismissal without understanding what the concerns actually are. This guide exists to support informed decision-making, not to advocate for any particular position.

"I tried ChatGPT once and it was rubbish."

It may have been. Early models (2023) were substantially less capable than current systems, free tiers use weaker models than paid subscriptions, and a single interaction without knowing how to prompt effectively is not a representative test. This is not to say current systems are reliable — they aren't, in important ways — but a brief unsatisfying encounter two years ago is not a sound basis for a settled position. The Getting Started page offers a 15-minute exercise designed to give a fairer impression. You may still conclude it's not useful for your work, and that's a perfectly valid outcome.

"Do I have to learn to code?"

No. The chat interfaces require no technical skills. Some advanced applications (batch processing, custom tools) involve programming, but these are entirely optional and most colleagues will never need them. What does help is learning to prompt effectively — being specific about what you want, providing context, iterating on responses. This is a communication skill, not a technical one. The prompting principles section covers the basics.

"What about data protection and GDPR?"

Commercial LLMs process data on external servers. Don't input personal data about identifiable individuals (student information, personnel matters, patient data) into any commercial LLM, regardless of privacy settings. For research data, check your institution's policy — many universities have or are developing guidance on AI use with research data. Most platforms offer settings to prevent your conversations being used for model training; enable these. For sensitive materials, consider whether the work can be done with anonymised data, or whether local/self-hosted models (which keep data on your own machine) are more appropriate. Your institution's data protection officer can advise on specific cases.
