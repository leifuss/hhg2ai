Large Language Models: A Guide for Humanities Scholars

For colleagues in Classics, Ancient History, and Theology & Religion

Last updated: February 2026

This guide aims to help you make informed decisions about whether and how to engage with Large Language Models. It is not advocacy for adoption. Some colleagues will find these tools useful for specific tasks; others will reasonably conclude they are not worth the trade-offs. Both positions are defensible.

The guide is designed for self-paced exploration. Start wherever suits your situation.

Where to start

If you're sceptical but curious, start with the Briefing. It covers what these systems actually do, common misconceptions in both directions, and the ethical dimensions worth considering.

If you want to try something now, go straight to Getting Started. There's a 15-minute exercise designed for scholars in our disciplines.

If you work with ancient languages, the Ancient Languages and Texts page shows worked translation examples in Latin, Greek, and Hebrew — presented without editorial judgment so you can assess the quality yourself.

If you have a specific concern, the FAQ addresses the questions colleagues most commonly raise.

The guide

Briefing

What LLMs are, what they aren't, ethical dimensions, and why informed awareness matters regardless of whether you use these tools. The foundation for everything else in this guide.

Getting Started

A 15-minute hands-on exercise, practical setup guidance (platforms, privacy settings), and core prompting principles. The best starting point if you want to experiment.

Mindset

AI requires a different approach from other technology. Foundational shifts in how to think about these tools, and practical techniques for getting better results — most of which draw on skills you already have.

Use Cases by Time Investment

Practical applications organised by how much time they require — from 15-minute tasks to deeper explorations. Each includes risks to watch for.

Ancient Languages and Texts

Worked translation examples in Latin, Greek, and Hebrew. How LLMs handle textual criticism, fragmentary evidence, and sacred texts. Written for specialists.

Agentic AI

How AI is moving from chat to autonomous tools that plan, code, and execute multi-step tasks. What agentic tools are, how to use them, and what has been built with them.

Microsoft Copilot

Best practice for your institution’s default AI tool. What Copilot does well, where it falls short, and how to get the most out of it — including when to use other tools instead.

Frequently Asked Questions

Direct answers to common concerns: replacement anxieties, student cheating, ethical questions, data protection, and more.

Glossary

AI terminology explained without unnecessary technicality. A reference to consult when you encounter unfamiliar jargon.

Resources

Curated reading list for humanities academics: sector guidance, research integrity, source-facing AI tools, pedagogy, and critical literacy. Exeter-specific links included.

About this guide

This guide was developed by Leif Isaksen, with assistance from Claude (Anthropic). It accompanies a workshop on AI for humanities scholars but is designed to be useful independently.

The content reflects the state of these technologies as of early 2026. LLM capabilities change rapidly; specific model names and features may be outdated by the time you read this. The broader principles — about verification, ethical awareness, and informed decision-making — should remain relevant longer than any particular technical detail.

A note on how this was made: Using AI to produce a guide about AI is itself a demonstration of the technology in action — including its limitations. The human author is responsible for all editorial decisions, factual claims, and errors.

For examples of what can be built with these tools, see the Claude AI Experiments portfolio.
